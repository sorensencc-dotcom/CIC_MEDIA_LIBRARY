Interview Transcript: Charles Emil Sorensen
Date: June 7, 2026
Duration: Approximately 45 minutes

Interviewer: Charles, thank you for taking the time to speak with us today. I'd like to start with your early years. What can you tell us about your childhood and early career in Denmark?

Charles: Well, I was born in Odense, Denmark in 1881. My father worked in the construction trade, and I learned engineering from him. We lived a modest life, but Odense was a vibrant cultural center. I attended technical school and became quite interested in the emerging automotive and manufacturing industries. When I was about 18 years old, in 1899, my family decided to emigrate to America. I had heard that there were tremendous opportunities in the industrial centers, particularly in Detroit. So I left Denmark and came to the United States alone initially.

Interviewer: And when did you arrive in Detroit?

Charles: I arrived in Detroit around 1900. I started working in small machine shops, learning English on the job. By 1902, I had secured a position at the Olds Motor Works. This was an exciting time in Detroit. I could see the potential in the automobile industry, though Henry Ford wasn't yet the dominant figure he would become.

Interviewer: Let's talk about your time at Ford. You became Chief of Production, correct?

Charles: Yes, that's right. I joined Ford Motor Company in 1911, and by 1921, I had become Chief of Production. I worked closely with Henry Ford on the assembly line innovations. But I want to emphasize something about Willow Run. During 1941 and 1942, we were building the B-24 Liberator bombers for the war effort. Willow Run was unlike anything the world had ever seen. We had 42,000 workers at peak production. The scale was extraordinary. I oversaw the production planning and logistics. We were producing a complete bomber every 63 minutes at our peak. It was the most intense manufacturing challenge I ever faced. Henry Ford entrusted me with enormous responsibility during that period.

Interviewer: That's remarkable. And then you left Ford in 1944?

Charles: Yes. I departed from Ford Motor Company in 1944. It was a difficult decision after more than 30 years with the company. There were differences in philosophy with the younger generation at Ford, particularly with Edsel's son. I felt my approach to manufacturing was no longer as valued. So I took a position with Willys-Overland in Toledo, Ohio.

Interviewer: Tell us about your post-Ford career at Willys-Overland from 1944 to 1968.

Charles: Well, I spent 24 years at Willys-Overland, from 1944 until my retirement in 1968. I was Vice President of Manufacturing. Willys was smaller than Ford, but it gave me the opportunity to apply everything I had learned. We were producing the Willys Jeep, which had become very popular after the war. I enjoyed the entrepreneurial atmosphere at Willys. We innovated in production methods and worker training. I traveled extensively during those years - to Overland's facilities in South America and Europe. I trained many younger engineers in mass production techniques. It was a satisfying chapter of my life, though not as prominent as my Ford years, of course. I retired in 1968 at the age of 87.

Interviewer: Were there any documents or photographs from that period that you've kept?

Charles: Yes, actually. I have some photographs from the Willow Run facility - some of them quite remarkable. And I have correspondence with several colleagues from Ford and Willys. I also have my personal journals from 1941 through the war years, where I recorded production figures and observations about the manufacturing challenges we faced.

Interviewer: Those would be invaluable for researchers. We would love to see those if you're willing to share them with the documentary project.

Charles: I think that would be appropriate. The story of American manufacturing during that era is important, and Willow Run was a remarkable achievement. I'm proud to contribute those materials to the historical record.
